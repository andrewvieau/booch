Continuous Kombucha Brewing and Kegging


Brewing Kombucha continuously means to retain the SCOBY and a portion of the final brew (starter) to be used in the next batch. This example and following list of equipment will help you to brew 5 gallons of kombucha continuously using a 6.5 gallon fermenter. You may adjust the volume of liquid or size of kettles depending on the resources you have available to you or your targeted batch size. I have had consistent (Amazing!!) results over hundreds of batches using the equipment below plus 6 cups of raw organic sugar and anywhere from 5 ounces to 10 ounces of loose leaf tea. Using all organic ingredients my average batch cost is about $2 per gallon.


On the day you deem it to be good then bottle or keg ⅘ of the volume and brew the same amount of sweet tea to replace it. For this example my target volume is 6.5 gallons so I would want to retain 1.5 gallons of finished starter in my fermenter to start the next batch. I keg my Kombucha in 5 gallon kegs and find that a 6.5 gallon fermenter is the perfect size to leave enough starter behind and still fill my 5 gallon keg. I recommend taste testing after 7 days and kegging or bottling when it reaches your desired level of tartness.


Ingredients

Here is what you will need:

  • 5 Gallon Stainless Steel Pot: Our Favorite | Budget
  • 3 Gallon Stainless Steel Pot: Our Favorite | Budget
  • Strainer: Our Favorite | Budget
  • 6 gallon brewing bucket: Our Favorite | Budget
  • 5-10 ounces of loose leaf tea = 40-80 tea bags: Our Favorite | Budget
    Quantity depends on desired flavor. More tea = stronger tea flavor.
  • 6 cups of sugar: Our Favorite | Budget

Brewing the Sweet Tea

  1. In the 3 gallon stainless steel kettle, boil 2 gallons of filtered or chlorine-free water.
    NOTE: Bacteria does not like chlorine so you must remove it by filtering or allowing water to stand for 24 hours so chlorine will evaporate
  2. Once the water is boiling, remove kettle from the heat and pitch in tea/tea bags.
  3. Steep for 10 minutes.
  4. While the tea water is steeping, add the 6 cups of sugar and put into the EMPTY 5 gallon kettle (NOT the 3 gallon kettle).
  5. After the 10 minute steep, strain the steeped tea into the 5 gallon kettle containing the sugar.
  6. Stir sugar with spatula until completely dissolved.
  7. Place 5 gallon kettle into sink and fill with cold filtered water or chlorine-free water.

Fermentation

  1. Add 1.5 gallons of fully fermented Kombucha starter with mother SCOBY to the EMPTY 6.5 gallon fermenter and place on heating pad/seedling heat mat.
  2. Make sure sugar/tea mixture in 5 gallon kettle is cool enough to touch. Pour tea into the 6.5 gallon fermenter.
  3. Cover fermenter with a T-shirt using a rubber band to secure, making sure there are no gaps for fruit flies to get in.
  4. Let ferment for 7 days.
  5. Start sample tasting daily on the 7th day until the desired taste is achieved.

Transfer to Keg

  1. Once desired flavor is achieved, wash your corny keg with a plant based soap (NOT Dawn).
  2. Add 2 tablespoons white vinegar to keg, secure lid, and shake keg to sanitize. Discard vinegar.
  3. Using the spigot on the 6.5 gallon fermenter, transfer to the 5 gallon corny keg (NOTE: important to pay attention to this part so you do not overflow the keg. It happens, trust me! ☺).
  4. Leave 1.5 gallons in the 6.5 gallon fermenter. This will be your starter (like in step 10) for your next brew.
  5. Secure Corny Keg lid, move to refrigerator with gas line installed then connect to Co2 gas. Set regulator to 20 psi and check for carbonation within 24 hours it may take longer so check often after 24 hours. Once desired carbonation has been reached turn off gas to keg. You may need to turn gas on for 20 seconds periodically to recharge keg always leaving gas off to conserve gas in-case of leaks.
  6. Leave 1.5 gallons in the 6.5 gallon fermenter. This will be your starter (like in step 10) for your next brew.
  7. Repeat steps 1-10 on the same day of transfer for a continuous brew.
